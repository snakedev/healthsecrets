## Remedy Evidence Profiles

### 1. St. John's Wort (*Hypericum perforatum*)

**Grade: A — Strong evidence for mild-to-moderate depression**

A flowering herb with active compounds hypericin and hyperforin that increase serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine — similar to conventional SSRIs.

| Study | Design | Findings |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Apaydin et al., 2016 [1] | Systematic review, 35 RCTs | Comparable to SSRIs for mild-moderate depression; fewer side effects |
| Linde et al., 1996 [2] | Meta-analysis, 23 RCTs | 2.67× more effective than placebo; similar to standard antidepressants |
| Szegedi et al., 2005 [3] | Double-blind RCT vs sertraline | Non-inferior to sertraline; superior responder rate |
| Gastpar et al., 2006 [4] | Double-blind RCT vs citalopram | Comparable to citalopram for moderate depression |

**Dosing:** 900mg/day divided (300mg 3× daily), standardized to 0.3% hypericin. Allow 4–6 weeks for full effect.

> ⚠️ **CRITICAL DRUG INTERACTIONS — CYP450 enzyme inducer:**
> SSRIs/SNRIs (serotonin syndrome), birth control (reduced effectiveness), warfarin (clotting risk), immunosuppressants (organ rejection), HIV antiretrovirals (viral rebound), chemotherapy (reduced effectiveness). **Consult a pharmacist before combining with ANY medication.**

**Not For:** Severe depression, bipolar disorder, pregnancy, patients on multiple medications. Side effects: photosensitivity, GI upset, fatigue. Discontinue 2 weeks before surgery.

### 2. SAMe (S-Adenosylmethionine)

**Grade: B — Good evidence as monotherapy and augmentation**

A naturally occurring methyl donor critical for neurotransmitter synthesis (serotonin, dopamine, norepinephrine).

| Study | Design | Findings |
|-------|--------|----------|
| De Berardis et al., 2020 [5] | Systematic review | Positive results as monotherapy and adjunct |
| Papakostas et al., 2010 [6] | Double-blind RCT | Significant improvement when added to SSRIs |
| Hardy et al., 2003 [7] | Meta-analysis, 14 studies | Equivalent efficacy to tricyclic antidepressants |
| Mischoulon & Fava, 2002 [8] | Review | Effective at 200–1,600mg/day |

**Dosing:** Start 400mg/day (morning, empty stomach), increase to 800–1,600mg/day. Enteric-coated tablets. Onset: 1–2 weeks (faster than SSRIs).

⚠️ **NOT for bipolar disorder** — can trigger mania (serious risk). Side effects: GI upset, anxiety, insomnia if taken evening. Cost: $30–60/month.

### 3. Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA/DHA)

**Grade: A — Strong evidence, especially EPA-dominant formulas**

Essential fatty acids with anti-inflammatory, neuroprotective, and neurotransmitter-supporting properties. EPA has the strongest antidepressant evidence.

| Study | Design | Findings |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Liao et al., 2019 [9] | Meta-analysis, 26 RCTs | EPA ≥60% formulas significantly reduced depression |
| Mocking et al., 2016 [10] | Meta-regression | EPA dosage positively correlated with effect |
| Hallahan et al., 2016 [11] | Systematic review | Significant benefit for diagnosed depression |

**Dosing:** 1–2g EPA/day (look for ≥60% EPA formulas). Take with meals for absorption. IFOS-certified for purity (mercury, PCBs, oxidation testing).

**Safety:** Generally well-tolerated. Blood thinning effect at high doses (caution with anticoagulants). GI effects: fishy aftertaste (enteric-coated helps).

**Best For:** All depression types as complementary support — particularly beneficial when inflammation is elevated.

### 4. Vitamin D

**Grade: B — Good evidence, especially for deficiency-related depression**

A fat-soluble hormone with receptors throughout the brain, particularly mood-regulating regions. Approximately 42% of US adults are deficient.

| Study | Design | Findings |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Anglin et al., 2013 [12] | Meta-analysis, 31,424 participants | Low vitamin D = significantly higher depression risk |
| Spedding, 2014 [13] | Meta-analysis of RCTs | Positive effect when biological deficiency present |
| Jorde et al., 2008 [14] | RCT, 441 adults | Significant improvement with supplementation |

**Dosing:** 2,000–4,000 IU/day (aim for 40–60 ng/mL blood levels). Test 25-hydroxyvitamin D first. Take with fat. Vitamin D3 preferred over D2. Sunlight: 15–30 min midday when possible.

**Safety:** Toxicity risk at very high doses (>10,000 IU/day without testing). Monitor calcium levels. Essential in winter and for indoor workers.

### 5. Saffron (*Crocus sativus*)

**Grade: B — Good evidence for mild-to-moderate depression**

Active compounds safranal and crocin increase serotonin and dopamine with anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects.

| Study | Design | Findings |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Hausenblas et al., 2013 [15] | Meta-analysis, 5 RCTs | Superior to placebo; comparable to fluoxetine and imipramine |
| Akhondzadeh et al., 2005 [16] | Double-blind RCT | 30mg saffron as effective as 20mg fluoxetine |
| Mazidi et al., 2016 [17] | Meta-analysis | Significant reduction in depression scores |

**Dosing:** 30mg/day standardized extract (safranal + crocin). 4–6 weeks for full effect. Use standardized extract, not culinary saffron.

**Safety:** Well-tolerated with minimal side effects. Cost: $20–40/month. Pregnancy: high doses (>5g) are toxic — avoid during pregnancy.

### 6. 5-HTP (5-Hydroxytryptophan)

**Grade: C — Moderate evidence; significant safety concerns**

Direct serotonin precursor that crosses the blood-brain barrier. Converts: 5-HTP → serotonin → melatonin.

| Study | Design | Findings |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Shaw et al., 2002 [18] | Cochrane review | Limited evidence suggests improvement |
| Jangid et al., 2013 [19] | Open-label comparison | Comparable efficacy to fluoxetine |

**Dosing:** Start 50mg/day (evening — promotes sleep via melatonin). Therapeutic: 100–300mg/day. Short-term use (3–6 months). Empty stomach for absorption.

> ⚠️ **SEROTONIN SYNDROME RISK — NEVER combine with:**
> - SSRIs (fluoxetine, sertraline, escitalopram, etc.)
> - SNRIs (venlafaxine, duloxetine, etc.)
> - MAOIs or tramadol
>
> **Symptoms:** Agitation, confusion, rapid heart rate, high blood pressure, muscle rigidity, seizures. **This is a medical emergency — call 911.**

### 7. Rhodiola Rosea

**Grade: B — Good evidence for stress-related depression and fatigue**

Adaptogenic herb modulating HPA axis stress response. Active compounds: rosavins, salidroside.

| Study | Design | Findings |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Mao et al., 2015 [20] | Double-blind RCT vs sertraline | Fewer side effects; modest antidepressant effect |
| Darbinyan et al., 2007 [21] | Phase III trial | Significant improvement in depression and insomnia |
| Gao et al., 2020 [22] | RCT, rhodiola + sertraline | Combination improved outcomes |

**Dosing:** 340–680mg/day, standardized 3% rosavins / 1% salidroside. Morning or early afternoon (mildly stimulating — avoid evening). Onset: 1–2 weeks.

**Best For:** Depression with fatigue, stress-related depression, when faster onset is desired.

### 8. Ashwagandha (*Withania somnifera*)

**Grade: B — Good evidence for stress-related depression and anxiety**

Adaptogenic herb reducing cortisol approximately 30% with GABAergic (calming) activity. Active compounds: withanolides.

| Study | Design | Findings |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Chandrasekhar et al., 2012 [23] | Double-blind RCT | 30% cortisol reduction; reduced anxiety and stress |
| Lopresti et al., 2019 [24] | Double-blind RCT | Significant improvements in stress, anxiety, depression |
| Salve et al., 2019 [25] | Prospective study | KSM-66 improved stress and sleep quality |

**Dosing:** 300–600mg/day KSM-66 or Sensoril extract. 4–8 weeks for full effect. Can be taken morning or evening (calming, not stimulating).

**Safety:** Well-tolerated. ⚠️ May increase thyroid hormones (caution with hyperthyroidism/thyroid medications). Avoid during pregnancy.

**Best For:** Stress-related depression, anxiety-depression comorbidity, chronic stress with elevated cortisol.

## Drug Interaction Matrix

| If You Take... | Avoid/Caution With | Why |
|----------------|-------------------|-----|
| **SSRIs/SNRIs** | ⚠️ St. John's Wort, 5-HTP | Serotonin syndrome (life-threatening) |
| **Blood thinners (Warfarin)** | St. John's Wort, omega-3 (high dose) | Altered anticoagulant effect |
| **Birth control pills** | ⚠️ St. John's Wort | Reduced contraceptive effectiveness |
| **Immunosuppressants** | ⚠️ St. John's Wort | Reduced drug levels (organ rejection risk) |
| **HIV antiretrovirals** | ⚠️ St. John's Wort | Reduced drug levels (viral rebound) |
| **Bipolar medications** | ⚠️ SAMe, St. John's Wort | Can trigger mania |
| **Thyroid medications** | Ashwagandha | May alter thyroid hormone levels |
| **Sedatives/benzodiazepines** | Ashwagandha | Enhanced sedation |
| **Chemotherapy** | ⚠️ St. John's Wort | Reduced drug effectiveness |

**General Rules:**
1. **Always consult your doctor** before combining natural remedies with medications
2. Space supplements 2 hours from medications
3. Stop herbal supplements 2 weeks before surgery
4. Start one supplement at a time to identify effects
5. Report all supplements to your healthcare provider

## FAQ

**Q: What are the most effective natural remedies for depression?**

**A:** The strongest evidence supports St. John's wort (900mg/day, comparable to SSRIs for mild-moderate depression), SAMe (800–1,600mg/day), omega-3 fatty acids (1–2g EPA/day), and saffron (30mg/day). Exercise at 150 minutes per week also has comparable efficacy to antidepressants for mild-moderate depression.

**Q: Can St. John's wort replace antidepressants?**

**A:** It has comparable efficacy to SSRIs for mild-to-moderate depression with fewer side effects. However, it has critical drug interactions with SSRIs, birth control, blood thinners, and many other medications via CYP450 enzyme induction. Not recommended for severe depression. Always consult a doctor first.

**Q: Is 5-HTP safe to take with antidepressants?**

**A:** No. Combining 5-HTP with SSRIs, SNRIs, or MAOIs can cause serotonin syndrome — a potentially life-threatening condition with agitation, confusion, rapid heart rate, and muscle rigidity. Never combine without medical supervision.

**Q: How long do natural antidepressants take to work?**

**A:** SAMe and rhodiola may show effects in 1–2 weeks. St. John's wort, saffron, and ashwagandha require 4–6 weeks. Omega-3s may take 4–8 weeks. Consistency is key — give each remedy adequate time before evaluating.

**Q: Which omega-3 is better for depression — EPA or DHA?**

**A:** EPA appears more effective. Meta-analyses show greatest benefit from formulas with ≥60% EPA relative to DHA. Typical dose: 1–2g EPA/day. DHA has protective effects but less direct antidepressant activity.

**Q: When should someone seek professional help instead of using natural remedies?**

**A:** Immediately for suicidal thoughts (call 988), severe impairment, or psychosis. Also if natural remedies haven't helped after 8–12 weeks, symptoms worsen, you have bipolar disorder, or depression is moderate-to-severe. Severe depression requires medication and therapy.
