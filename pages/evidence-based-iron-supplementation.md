## Quick Answer {#quick-answer}

> **Iron supplement protocols depend on your deficiency stage, tolerance, and absorption capacity.** Key facts:
> - **Ferrous bisglycinate** is the best-tolerated form with comparable absorption to ferrous sulfate but 60–70% fewer GI side effects [2]
> - **Alternate-day dosing** improves fractional iron absorption by ~33% compared to daily dosing due to hepcidin regulation [3]
> - **Vitamin C (100–200 mg)** taken with iron increases absorption 3–4x — the single most impactful optimization [4]
> - **Never supplement iron without testing** — ferritin, serum iron, TIBC, and transferrin saturation define your protocol

## Which Iron Form Is Best for You?

**Ferrous bisglycinate (chelated iron) is the best first-line choice for most people** — a 2014 randomized trial in the *Journal of Perinatal Medicine* found 25 mg of bisglycinate matched the efficacy of 50 mg ferrous sulfate for preventing iron deficiency, with significantly fewer GI complaints (P=0.001) [2]. Budget-conscious individuals who tolerate GI effects well may prefer ferrous sulfate, which remains the most clinically studied form.

The key distinction is **ferrous vs. ferric** iron. Ferrous (Fe²⁺) forms are significantly better absorbed because the intestinal transporter DMT1 preferentially absorbs iron in its reduced state. Ferric (Fe³⁺) iron must first be converted by stomach acid — an extra step that lowers bioavailability and makes ferric forms unsuitable for oral supplementation [5].

### How Does Chelation Change Iron Absorption?

**Chelated iron (bisglycinate) bypasses several absorption barriers** that limit traditional ferrous salts. The two glycine molecules bonded to the iron atom protect it through the acidic stomach environment and enable absorption through amino acid transporters in addition to the standard DMT1 channel [6]. This dual-pathway absorption means bisglycinate is less affected by dietary inhibitors like phytates and tannins.

A study in Cambodian women showed that 18 mg bisglycinate was not equivalent to 60 mg ferrous sulfate — suggesting that when very low doses are used, the form advantage doesn't fully compensate for the dose gap [7]. The practical takeaway: bisglycinate shines at equivalent elemental iron doses, not at dramatically lower ones.

## How Does Hepcidin Affect Iron Absorption?

**Hepcidin is the master regulator of iron homeostasis — and it's why daily iron dosing may be less efficient than alternate-day dosing.** When you take an oral iron dose, your liver releases hepcidin within hours, blocking the ferroportin export channel on intestinal cells for approximately 24 hours [3]. A second dose taken the next day faces a significantly higher absorption barrier.

### The ETH Zurich Alternate-Day Discovery

Groundbreaking research from ETH Zurich (Stoffel et al., *Haematologica* 2020) demonstrated that **alternate-day iron dosing produced 33% higher fractional iron absorption** compared to consecutive-day dosing in women with iron deficiency anemia [3]. The study used labeled iron isotopes to precisely measure absorption.

A follow-up randomized double-blind placebo-controlled trial (von Siebenthal et al., *eClinicalMedicine* 2023) compared 90 consecutive days of 100 mg iron vs. the same total dose spread over 180 alternate days [8]. Key results:

- **Equal ferritin repletion** at 3 months (43.8 vs 44.8 μg/L, P=0.98)
- **Significantly fewer GI side effects** with alternate-day dosing (LPR 1.56, P<0.0001)
- **Lower iron deficiency prevalence at 6 months** with alternate-day dosing (3.0% vs 11.4%, P=0.049)
- **Lower serum hepcidin** in the alternate-day group (1.9 vs 3.0 nM, P<0.0001)

A 2024 JAMA Network Open study in US veterans with IDA further supported these findings, showing alternate-day dosing achieved a 34% higher absorption rate [9].

**Bottom line:** For non-urgent iron repletion, alternate-day dosing gives you the same ferritin recovery with fewer side effects. For severe anemia requiring rapid correction, daily dosing under medical supervision remains standard.

## What Iron Dosing Protocol Should You Follow?

**The correct iron dose depends on your deficiency stage, hemoglobin level, and tolerance.** A 2024 AGA Clinical Practice Update recommends starting with lower doses and using alternate-day scheduling when possible [11].

| Condition | Elemental Iron/Day | Duration | Preferred Form | Schedule |
|-----------|-------------------|----------|----------------|----------|
| **Iron deficiency (no anemia)** | 25–50 mg | 3–6 months | Bisglycinate | Alternate-day preferred |
| **Iron deficiency anemia** | 45–65 mg | 3–6 months minimum | Sulfate or bisglycinate | Daily or alternate-day |
| **Pregnancy (routine)** | 27–30 mg | Throughout pregnancy | Bisglycinate | Daily (in prenatal) |
| **Pregnancy (anemic)** | 60–120 mg | Medical supervision | Bisglycinate or sulfate | Daily, split doses |
| **Prevention (high-risk)** | 10–20 mg | Ongoing while at risk | Low-dose bisglycinate | Alternate-day |

### Critical Dosing Principles

1. **Elemental iron ≠ compound weight** — 325 mg ferrous sulfate = only 65 mg elemental iron
2. **Start low, go slow** — titrate up to minimize GI side effects
3. **Split large doses** — if taking >65 mg, divide into two doses 6+ hours apart
4. **Don't stop too early** — continue until ferritin >50 ng/mL, even after hemoglobin normalizes
5. **Tolerable Upper Intake Level** — 45 mg/day from supplements; therapeutic doses for anemia exceed this under medical supervision [12]

## What Enhances or Blocks Iron Absorption?

**Vitamin C is the single most impactful absorption enhancer — increasing non-heme iron uptake 3–4x** by reducing ferric iron to its absorbable ferrous form and creating soluble chelates [4].

### Absorption Enhancers

| Factor | Mechanism | Effect | Action |
|--------|-----------|--------|--------|
| **Vitamin C** | Reduces Fe³⁺ → Fe²⁺; chelates iron | 3–4x increase | Take 100–200 mg with iron |
| **Citric acid** | Similar reduction mechanism | Moderate increase | Consume citrus with iron |
| **MFP factor** | Meat/fish/poultry peptides | 2–3x increase | Pair plant iron with meat |
| **Empty stomach** | Max acid exposure | 2–3x vs. food | Take 1 hr before meals |

### Absorption Inhibitors

| Factor | Mechanism | Effect | Strategy |
|--------|-----------|--------|----------|
| **Calcium** | Competes for DMT1 | 50–60% decrease | Separate by 2+ hours |
| **Tannins (tea, coffee)** | Binds iron | 50–90% decrease | Avoid within 1–2 hrs |
| **Phytates (grains, legumes)** | Chelates iron | 50–65% decrease | Soak/sprout; separate timing |
| **PPIs/antacids** | Reduce stomach acid | 30–50% decrease | Separate by 2–4 hrs |
| **Polyphenols** | Bind iron | 20–50% decrease | Avoid with iron meals |

## How Do You Interpret Iron Lab Results?

**Ferritin is the single most important test** — it directly reflects iron stores. Caveat: ferritin is an acute-phase reactant that rises with inflammation, potentially masking deficiency [13].

### Complete Iron Panel Reference

| Test | Normal Range | Iron Deficiency | Iron Overload | Notes |
|------|-------------|-----------------|---------------|-------|
| **Serum Ferritin** | 30–300 ng/mL | <30 (depleted); <15 (severe) | >300 | Most important; optimal 50–100 |
| **Serum Iron** | 60–170 mcg/dL | Low | High | Varies with meals |
| **TIBC** | 240–450 mcg/dL | Increased | Decreased | Inversely correlates with stores |
| **Transferrin Sat.** | 20–50% | <20% | >50% | (Serum Iron / TIBC) × 100 |
| **Hemoglobin** | 12–16 (W); 13.5–17.5 (M) g/dL | Low (anemia) | Normal | Can be normal with depleted stores |
| **MCV** | 80–100 fL | Low (microcytic) | Normal | Small RBCs = chronic deficiency |

### Interpreting Results

- **Ferritin <30 + Normal Hb** = Stage 1–2 deficiency. Supplement 25–50 mg bisglycinate.
- **Ferritin <15 + Low Hb** = Iron deficiency anemia. Treat with 45–65 mg under medical guidance.
- **Ferritin >50 + Normal Hb** = Adequate. No supplementation needed.
- **Elevated ferritin + Low iron** = Likely inflammation masking deficiency. Request CRP and transferrin saturation.

## Who Is at Highest Risk for Iron Deficiency?

**Menstruating women, pregnant individuals, vegetarians, endurance athletes, and frequent blood donors face the highest risk** [1][14].

| Risk Group | Prevalence | Key Driver | Action |
|-----------|-----------|------------|--------|
| **Menstruating women** | 30–40% deficient | Monthly blood loss | Annual ferritin; supplement if <50 |
| **Pregnant women** | 50% without supplements | ↑ blood volume + fetal needs | Routine 27 mg/day |
| **Vegetarians/vegans** | 1.8x higher risk | Non-heme iron only | Monitor; pair with vitamin C |
| **Endurance athletes** | 15–35% | Sweat, GI loss, hemolysis | Biannual check; supplement if <50 |
| **Blood donors** | High | 200–250 mg per donation | Monitor; supplement between |
| **GI disorders** | High | Malabsorption + bleeding | May need IV iron |

## Step-by-Step Iron Supplementation Protocol

### Phase 1: Test (Week 1)
1. Request a **complete iron panel**: ferritin, serum iron, TIBC, transferrin saturation, Hb/CBC
2. Include **CRP** — elevated CRP can falsely raise ferritin
3. Fast 8–12 hours before draw for accurate serum iron
4. Record baseline numbers for tracking

### Phase 2: Select Form & Dose (Week 1–2)
1. **Ferritin <30, normal Hb:** Bisglycinate 25 mg every other day
2. **Ferritin <15, low Hb:** Bisglycinate 50 mg or sulfate 65 mg daily (medical supervision)
3. **Prevention (high-risk):** Bisglycinate 10–20 mg every other day
4. Purchase **vitamin C 100–200 mg** tablets to take alongside

### Phase 3: Optimize Absorption (Weeks 2–12)
1. Take iron with vitamin C on an **empty stomach** (1 hr before breakfast)
2. If GI issues: take with small low-calcium meal, or switch to bisglycinate
3. **Separate from calcium, tea, coffee** by 2+ hours
4. Consider **alternate-day dosing** for equal efficacy with fewer side effects [3][8]
5. **Manage constipation:** hydration, fiber, magnesium 300–400 mg if needed

### Phase 4: Retest & Adjust (Month 3)
1. Recheck ferritin, Hb, and complete iron panel
2. **Ferritin rising toward 50+:** Continue current protocol
3. **No improvement after 3 months:** Consult about dose increase, form change, or IV iron
4. **Ferritin >50, Hb normal:** Transition to maintenance or stop if no ongoing risk
5. Continue until ferritin >50 ng/mL — **don't stop when Hb normalizes**

## Frequently Asked Questions {#frequently-asked-questions}

**Q: Which form of iron supplement is best absorbed?**
**A:** Ferrous bisglycinate offers the best combination of absorption and tolerability. A 2014 trial found 25 mg bisglycinate matched 50 mg ferrous sulfate for preventing iron deficiency during pregnancy, with significantly fewer GI side effects [2].

**Q: Should you take iron every day or every other day?**
**A:** ETH Zurich research showed alternate-day dosing produced 33% higher fractional iron absorption. Oral iron triggers a hepcidin spike lasting 24 hours that blocks further absorption. Alternate-day also causes fewer GI side effects [3][8].

**Q: What ferritin level indicates iron deficiency?**
**A:** Serum ferritin below 30 ng/mL indicates depleted stores. Many experts consider below 50 ng/mL suboptimal, especially with symptoms like fatigue or brain fog. Optimal for most adults is 50–100 ng/mL [13].

**Q: Does vitamin C really help iron absorption?**
**A:** Yes — vitamin C increases non-heme iron absorption 3–4x by reducing ferric iron to the absorbable ferrous form and creating soluble chelates. Take 100–200 mg with your iron supplement [4].

**Q: How long does it take for iron supplements to work?**
**A:** Energy improves within 2–4 weeks. Hemoglobin rises within 4–8 weeks. Full iron store repletion (ferritin >50 ng/mL) requires 3–6 months of consistent supplementation, even after hemoglobin normalizes [11].

**Q: Why do iron supplements cause constipation?**
**A:** Iron slows gut motility and hardens stool, especially ferrous sulfate above 45 mg [10]. Switch to bisglycinate for dramatic improvement. Also: increase water, add fiber, and consider magnesium 300–400 mg.

**Q: Can you take iron and calcium at the same time?**
**A:** No — calcium strongly inhibits iron absorption by competing for DMT1 transporters. Separate iron and calcium (including dairy) by at least 2 hours [4].

This repository is for educational purposes only. The information provided does not constitute medical advice. Never begin iron supplementation without first confirming deficiency through blood testing ordered by a qualified healthcare provider. Keep all iron supplements out of reach of children.
